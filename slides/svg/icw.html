Logical Fallacies

Evidence - Burden of Proof

That which can be asserted without evidence can be dismissed without evidence
--Euclid

"Don't you believe in flying saucers, they ask me? Don't you believe in telepathy? — in ancient astronauts? — in the Bermuda triangle? — in life after death? No, I reply. No, no, no, no, and again no. One person recently, goaded into desperation by the litany of unrelieved negation, burst out "Don't you believe in anything?" Yes", I said. "I believe in evidence. I believe in observation, measurement, and reasoning, confirmed by independent observers. I'll believe anything, no matter how wild and ridiculous, if there is evidence for it. The wilder and more ridiculous something is, however, the firmer and more solid the evidence will have to be."
― Isaac Asimov

Anectodal Evidence

An anecdote is a single story or example. It is the worst form of evidence because one example doesn't prove anything. It cannot be used to extrapolate out to a larger population any kind of predictable theory. Anecdotes can be used for illustrative purposes or to get people interested in the topic. However, it isn't good evidence.

Statistical Evidence

This is the best form of evidence, so long as certain methods are followed.

  1. Subjects need to be selected at random from the general population. Volunteers are biased.
  2. Subjects need to be blind as to whom is receiving the treatment.
  3. Subjects need to come from a large sample size.
  4. Half of the subjects should be treated and the other half treated with a placebo.
  5. If statistics come from survey questions, survey questions should not lead the subject.

Presenting statistics

  1. Statistics should be presented as both raw numbers and ratios or percentages.

  2. Raw numbers provide a sense of scale.
  3. Percentages and ratios provide context: 70% of America's debt is owed to itself.
  4. A percentage is a number out of 100: 48% of people surveyed, 23% of all students.
  5. A ratio is a number out of another number: 3 out of 5 dentists, 1 in 10 babies.

Quotations

Quotations provide evidence when they come from actual experts and not self-appointed experts. So, who is an expert?

  1. Experts are people who have 10,000 hours of effortful practice in their field.
  2. Experts have proven their expertise through practice or publishing in a peer-reviewed journal

Analogies

Analogies are comparisons between two similar things. Similarities should be pointed out.

  1. Repeat - Two similar things
  2. Repeat - Two similar things
  3. They are often used to stoke emotion and anger.
    1. "Assault" Weapons
    2. Abortion = Genocide
    3. Death Tax

Grammar and Usage

Point of View

  1. First person — I, me, my, we, us, myself
  2. Second person — your, yourself, yours
  3. Third person — They, them, it, she, him, her, he, Jeff, Clarisse

Subject — Verb Agreement

  1. Subjects and verbs must agree with one another in number.
  2. Plural subjects get plural verbs — Singular subjects get singular verbs

Plural subjects

  1. Subjects that end in "s".
    1. cars, buses, teams, dogs, cats
  2. Compound subjects combined with "and".
